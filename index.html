This is our Gesture-Controlled Robotic Arm - designed to translate real-time human hand movements into robotic motion. We use MPU6050 to detect hand orientation and gestures. The sensor data is processed by Arduino ESP32 microcontroller, which sends precise control signals to the servo motors on the arm. Our goal is to create an intuitive human‚Äìmachine interface, so controlling the robot feels as natural as moving your own hand. This technology has applications in industrial automation, medical robotics, and hazardous environment handling.

üìÑ Abstract

This paper presents the development of a gesture-controlled robotic arm that enables intuitive, real-time control through natural hand movements. The system utilises a wearable glove embedded with flex sensors and an MPU6050 accelerometer to detect finger bending and hand orientation. Sensor data is processed by an ESP32 microcontroller on the glove, which wirelessly transmits control commands using the ESP-NOW protocol to a second ESP32 mounted on the robotic arm. The robotic arm, designed with multiple degrees of freedom, is actuated by servo motors that replicate the user's hand gestures with accuracy and responsiveness. The design aims to provide a hands-free, user-friendly alternative to traditional control interfaces like joysticks or keyboards. With its low cost and ease of use, the system shows strong potential for applications in assistive technologies, industrial automation, remote manipulation, and medical robotics. This work demonstrates the effectiveness of integrating wearable sensor systems with embedded wireless control to enhance human‚Äìrobot interaction.

‚ùì Problem

Traditionally, robotic arms are operated using physical input devices like buttons, joysticks, or computer interfaces. While these methods are effective, they often require physical contact, user training, and may not be suitable in environments where safety, hygiene, or accessibility are critical. To overcome these limitations, gesture control has emerged as a more natural and intuitive alternative. With gesture-based systems, users can control robotic devices through simple hand movements, offering a more contactless and user-friendly experience.

üí° Solution

A gesture-controlled system with wearable glove sensors and ESP32 modules provides natural, real-time, and wireless control of robotic arms, making them user-friendly and versatile.

üè† Product and Output

The gesture-controlled robotic arm was evaluated under multiple test conditions to measure its accuracy, responsiveness, and reliability. Testing focused on correct gesture interpretation, servo precision, communication latency, and consistent performance over extended use. The system achieved an average gesture recognition accuracy of 95%, with reliable detection of finger bending across various hand sizes after minor calibration. The MPU6050 sensor accurately tracked wrist orientation, enabling intuitive control of arm tilt and rotation.


Real-time gesture recognition and motor response were achieved with minimal delay, averaging under 30 milliseconds using the ESP-NOW protocol. The robotic arm successfully performed tasks such as picking, rotating, and positioning lightweight objects at predefined locations. MG995 servos provided adequate torque for smooth base and shoulder movement, while SG90 micro servos managed finer motions in the wrist and gripper. Minor oscillations were observed during rapid movements, mostly due to limitations of the smaller servos.


The system proved stable during continuous operation, maintaining signal quality and sensor data consistency for over 30 minutes. Its modular structure also simplified hardware adjustments and part replacement during troubleshooting. As the design relies on sensor input rather than vision systems, performance remained largely unaffected by external factors like ambient lighting or electromagnetic interference. Some minor signal noise was noted in the flex sensor output, which could be mitigated in future versions with enhanced filtering or smoothing algorithms.
